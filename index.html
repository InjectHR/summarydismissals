Core concept

What summary dismissal means

Summary dismissal means ending employment immediately, without notice or payment in lieu of notice, because the conduct is serious enough to make continuing the employment relationship inappropriate.

The other pathway is termination with notice, or payment in lieu of notice. A key early question is not just “was this bad?”, but “is it so serious that notice would be inappropriate?”

The Fair Work starting point is Regulation 1.07: serious misconduct includes wilful or deliberate conduct inconsistent with continuing employment, conduct causing serious and imminent risk to safety or to the employer's reputation, viability or profitability, and examples such as theft, fraud, assault, sexual harassment, intoxication at work and refusing a lawful and reasonable instruction.

HR test before action

  • Has the conduct actually been established on evidence?
  • Is the conduct serious enough for immediate termination?
  • Is termination with notice a safer or more proportionate option?
  • Has the employee been told the allegation and possible consequence?
  • Has the employee had a real chance to respond, even if they admitted it or were caught red handed?
  • Has the business considered mitigation, consistency and proportionality?

Early pathway screen

Summary dismissal or termination with notice?

The usual alternative to summary dismissal is termination with notice, or payment in lieu of notice. Summary dismissal should be reserved for conduct that is so serious it would be inappropriate to continue the working relationship during a notice period.

Option A

Terminate with notice

Use this pathway where there may be a valid reason to end employment, but the conduct does not clearly justify immediate termination without notice. The business still needs a valid reason, consultation or procedural steps where required, and correct final pay.

Option B

Summary dismissal

Use this pathway only where the conduct is established, serious, proportionate to treat as immediate dismissal, and the employee has still been afforded procedural fairness.

Critical reminder

“Caught red handed” is not a process substitute

An admission, CCTV footage, a positive test, a confession, or strong witness evidence may help establish the conduct. It does not remove the need to notify the employee of the allegation, explain the possible consequence, and genuinely consider their response.

Common traps

Where summary dismissal goes wrong

01

Predetermined outcome

A meeting is not procedurally fair if the employee's response could never realistically change the decision.

02

Allegations too vague

The employee should understand the specific conduct relied on and why it may lead to disciplinary action, including dismissal.

03

Weak investigation file

HR should be able to point to evidence, witness accounts, policy clauses, and the steps taken before a decision was made.

04

Skipping proportionality

Even serious conduct should be weighed against context, consistency, length of service, safety impact, remorse and any mitigating circumstances.

05

Assuming no unfair dismissal access means no risk

Employees outside unfair dismissal access, such as some probationary employees or high-income employees who are not award or agreement covered, can still bring other claims, including general protections, discrimination, contract, WHS or underpayment-related claims.

06

Over-relying on the label “serious misconduct”

The Commission will look at the facts, evidence, process and proportionality. A serious misconduct label does not automatically make summary dismissal fair.

    Case reminders

    What the cases show

    These examples are designed to help HR compare facts, not to create automatic rules. The same category of conduct can produce different outcomes depending on the role, safety risk, policy clarity, evidence, mitigation and process.

    Examples where serious misconduct was found or dismissal was upheld

    Case Conduct Finding
    Szentpeteri v Serco Australia Pty Ltd [2024] FWC 1523 Security detention services officer found sleeping on duty at an immigration detention centre. Conduct was substantiated and characterised as serious misconduct. The role and safety/security context were central. Dismissal was not unfair.
    Saad v M.A Lisek & S.S Louth-Robins [2023] FWC 2704 Employee posted in a staff Facebook group using a highly offensive slur about the business owners/manager. The conduct was accepted as wilful, deliberate and inconsistent with ongoing employment. The dismissal complied with the Small Business Fair Dismissal Code.
    Josey v OS MCAP Pty Ltd [2024] FWC 2731 Alleged sexual harassment during fly-in/fly-out travel. There was a valid reason and the dismissal was not harsh, unjust or unreasonable. The employer's code treated sexual harassment as serious and capable of being a one-off incident.
    Dewar v Pek Care [2025] FWC 1587 Threatening comments during a heated meeting, including references to bikies and a bullet. The threats amounted to serious misconduct. Even without an intention to act on them, the threats justified summary dismissal under the Small Business Fair Dismissal Code.
    Aisa v Southern Cross Protection Pty Ltd [2025] FWC 1856 Security guard returned a positive methylamphetamine test in breach of a drug and alcohol policy. The policy was clear, understood and linked to safety and reputation. Breach of the policy was a valid reason and the application was dismissed.

    Examples where serious misconduct was not found, or summary dismissal was not justified

    Case Conduct alleged Finding
    Margaritis v Safiery Pty Ltd [2024] FWC 1844 Alleged insubordination for not calling the manager daily and allegedly failing to return customer calls. Serious misconduct was not found. There was no valid reason, concerns were not clearly communicated, evidence was inconclusive and the conduct was not serious enough.
    Witherden v DP World Sydney Ltd [2025] FWC 294 Stevedore tested positive for cocaine metabolites, breaching a drug and alcohol policy. There was a valid reason, but dismissal was still harsh and unreasonable, with reinstatement ordered. Misconduct does not automatically make dismissal fair.
    Lainas v Patrick Terminals [2024] FWC 311 Alleged aggressive/rude conduct, insubordination, incorrect timesheet and covert recordings. Some valid reasons existed, but the dismissal was unfair due to serious procedural deficiencies, including failure to properly particularise allegations and give a fair chance to respond.
    Walker v Plumbtrax Pty Ltd [2025] FWC 872; appeal refused [2025] FWCFB 219 Employee registered a similar business and had an Instagram account. Employer alleged competing business activity using company resources/time. Dismissal was not consistent with the Small Business Fair Dismissal Code and was harsh, unjust and unreasonable. The Full Bench noted no evidence of solicitation during employment.

    Slavin v Horizon Holdings Pty Ltd [2012] FWA 5588

    Predetermination undermined the process

    The Commission accepted there was a valid reason connected to failing to comply with lawful and reasonable directions about a company car. The dismissal was still found unfair because the employer had effectively decided the outcome before the disciplinary meeting, including reading from a script that ended in termination.

    Toolkit lesson: never run a response meeting as theatre. The employee's response must be capable of changing the outcome.

    Peter Jones v Exclusive Contracting (WA) Pty Ltd [2026] FWC 253

    Offensive conduct did not excuse process gaps

    The employee was summarily dismissed after racially offensive comments at a toolbox meeting. The Commission found a valid reason, but procedural fairness was not afforded because the employer had not properly notified him of the reason before deciding to dismiss.

    Toolkit lesson: even highly inappropriate conduct needs clear notification and a genuine chance to respond before the decision.

    Manager coaching

    Suggested language

    Keep the first disciplinary meeting focused on allegations, evidence and response. Avoid phrases that imply the outcome has already been locked in.

    Use

    "We are considering this matter and no final decision has been made."

    "This is your opportunity to respond to the allegations and provide any context."

    "We will consider your response before deciding the appropriate outcome."

    Avoid

    "We have decided to terminate you, but wanted to meet first."

    "This is just a formality."

    "Nothing you say will change the outcome."
