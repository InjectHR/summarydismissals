Core concept

What summary dismissal means

Summary dismissal means ending employment immediately, without notice or payment in lieu of notice, because the alleged conduct is serious enough to be inconsistent with the employment relationship continuing.

The internal fact sheet and Fair Work guidance both point to serious misconduct examples such as theft, fraud, assault, sexual harassment, intoxication at work, serious safety or business risk, and refusal to follow a lawful and reasonable instruction connected to the job.

HR test before action

  • Has the conduct actually been established on evidence?
  • Is the conduct serious enough for immediate termination?
  • Is the employee eligible to bring an unfair dismissal claim?
  • Has the employee been told the allegation and possible consequence?
  • Has the employee had a real chance to respond?
  • Has the business considered mitigation, consistency and proportionality?

Claim pathway screen

Unfair dismissal access is not the only risk

Some employees may not be protected from unfair dismissal, for example because they have not completed the minimum employment period, or because they earn above the current high income threshold and are not covered by an award or enterprise agreement.

That can change the unfair dismissal risk profile, but it should not be treated as permission to skip basic fairness, evidence or documentation. The business should still consider general protections, discrimination, breach of contract, underpayment, policy, WHS and reputational risks.

Check eligibility carefully

Confirm minimum employment period, small business status, award coverage, enterprise agreement coverage and the current high income threshold before relying on an exclusion.

General protections can still apply

A dismissal connected to a workplace right, complaint, protected attribute or prohibited reason can create risk even where unfair dismissal access is unavailable.

Keep the process proportionate

The process may be simpler for an employee outside unfair dismissal coverage, but HR should still establish the facts, avoid retaliatory reasons, and document the lawful basis.

Common traps

Where summary dismissal goes wrong

01

Predetermined outcome

A meeting is not procedurally fair if the employee's response could never realistically change the decision.

02

Allegations too vague

The employee should understand the specific conduct relied on and why it may lead to disciplinary action, including dismissal.

03

Weak investigation file

HR should be able to point to evidence, witness accounts, policy clauses, and the steps taken before a decision was made.

04

Skipping proportionality

Even serious conduct should be weighed against context, consistency, length of service, safety impact, remorse and any mitigating circumstances.

    Case reminders

    Valid reason can still be an unfair dismissal

    Slavin v Horizon Holdings Pty Ltd [2012] FWA 5588

    Predetermination undermined the process

    The Commission accepted there was a valid reason connected to failing to comply with lawful and reasonable directions about a company car. The dismissal was still found unfair because the employer had effectively decided the outcome before the disciplinary meeting, including reading from a script that ended in termination.

    Toolkit lesson: never run a response meeting as theatre. The employee's response must be capable of changing the outcome.

    Peter Jones v Exclusive Contracting (WA) Pty Ltd [2026] FWC 253

    Offensive conduct did not excuse process gaps

    The employee was summarily dismissed after racially offensive comments at a toolbox meeting. The Commission found a valid reason, but procedural fairness was not afforded because the employer had not properly notified him of the reason before deciding to dismiss.

    Toolkit lesson: even highly inappropriate conduct needs clear notification and a genuine chance to respond before the decision.

    Manager coaching

    Suggested language

    Keep the first disciplinary meeting focused on allegations, evidence and response. Avoid phrases that imply the outcome has already been locked in.

    Use

    "We are considering this matter and no final decision has been made."

    "This is your opportunity to respond to the allegations and provide any context."

    "We will consider your response before deciding the appropriate outcome."

    Avoid

    "We have decided to terminate you, but wanted to meet first."

    "This is just a formality."

    "Nothing you say will change the outcome."
